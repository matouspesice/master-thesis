## Primary source (working link) — 63 ms cybersickness

**Caserman, P., Martinussen, M., Göbel, S. (2019).** “Effects of End-to-end Latency on User Experience and Performance in Immersive Virtual Reality Applications.” In: *Entertainment Computing and Serious Games* (ICEC-JCSG 2019), Springer, pp. 57–69.

- **Working link (Springer):**  
  **https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-34644-7_5**
- **DOI:** 10.1007/978-3-030-34644-7_5

**Where the 63 ms appears:**  
- **Abstract:** “Results show that the end-to-end latency above 63 ms induces significant cybersickness symptoms. In addition, user performance decreases with increasing delay and with end-to-end latency above 69 ms, the users need significantly longer to complete the task. Results also show that end-to-end latency affects body ownership significant later, namely, not until 101 ms.”
- **Section 5 (Results)** and **Section 6 (Discussion)** give the detailed statistical results (SSQ for cybersickness, reaching task for performance, embodiment questionnaire for body ownership).

So: **63 ms** = cybersickness (SSQ); **69 ms** = performance (task time); **101 ms** = body ownership. Your thesis table also cites **75 ms** (motor performance / simultaneity) and **125 ms** (body ownership / agency) — those come from **Waltemate et al.**, which Caserman et al. cite in their Related Work (Sect. 2).
